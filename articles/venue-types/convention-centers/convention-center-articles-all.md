# Houston Is Betting $1.1 Billion That Its Convention Center Can Compete With Anyone

Houston has never lacked confidence when it comes to scale. The city that built one of the country's largest medical centers, hosts three major professional sports venues within a few blocks of its downtown core, and regularly lands Super Bowls and international summits is now placing a $1.1 billion wager that its convention infrastructure deserves to match the ambition.

Construction is underway on GRB Houston South, a new 700,000-square-foot building that will sit adjacent to the existing George R. Brown Convention Center and be directly connected to it. A 100,000-square-foot outdoor pedestrian plaza will tie the complex to Toyota Center, home of the Houston Rockets, creating a walkable entertainment and convention district that city leaders have been envisioning for years. The first phase broke ground in 2025, with completion targeted for 2028.

## What the Building Will Contain

The new structure is designed from the ground up as an events destination. It will house two exhibition halls, a multipurpose atrium that opens onto the new central plaza, flexible indoor and outdoor gathering spaces, and what is being billed as the largest ballroom in Texas. Ground-level retail and restaurant space will line the exterior, with the aim of activating the street life along the convention district's eastern edge.

The architecture draws a deliberate contrast with the George R. Brown's iconic ship-like exterior. Renderings show sustainable timber features, rooftop green spaces, and a more human-scaled pedestrian environment — a conscious effort to make the complex feel like part of the city rather than a fortress built to insulate convention-goers from it.

Design is being handled by Populous, the Kansas City firm behind numerous major sports and convention venues. Houston-based Hines is serving as development manager, with a joint venture of Gilbane and Flintco leading construction management.

## How Houston Is Paying for It

The financing structure is notable for what it doesn't rely on: local property taxes. The project is funded through hotel occupancy tax revenue — specifically, the incremental HOT receipts generated within a three-mile radius of the convention center — a mechanism made possible by Texas Senate Bill 1057, passed in 2023 and championed by then-State Senator, now Mayor, John Whitmire.

The City of Houston and Houston First Corporation closed on approximately $1.38 billion in bonds in late March and early April of 2026, with the deal drawing enough investor demand that all four series were oversubscribed. S&P upgraded the senior lien bonds from A to AA-, a signal of confidence in Houston's long-term fiscal position. The full master plan for the district carries a price tag approaching $2 billion, with the balance to be built out across additional phases through 2038.

## The Larger Ambition

The convention center expansion is timed alongside a period of unusual visibility for Houston on the world stage. The city is hosting FIFA World Cup matches this summer, including several group-stage games and a knockout-round fixture. The Republican National Convention is scheduled for Houston in 2028 — the same year GRB South is set to open.

Houston First Corporation projects the full build-out could generate more than $20 billion in economic activity over the next 30 years and increase events at the facility by roughly 30 percent annually. Whether those projections hold, the city's bet is straightforward: in a national convention market where Chicago, Las Vegas, and Orlando compete aggressively for the biggest events, Houston believes a state-of-the-art facility linked to three sports venues, a park, and a walkable hotel district is an argument no other city can quite replicate.

# Cleanliness Is No Longer a Back-of-House Concern at Convention Venues

The days when a convention center could win business on square footage and loading dock capacity alone are fading. A growing body of evidence from the meetings and events industry suggests that event planners are now asking a different set of questions before they sign a contract — and the answers hinge less on floor plans than on infection control protocols and sustainability credentials.

That shift is quantified in this year's Centers of Excellence program from EXHIBITOR Magazine, which recognizes the top-performing convention centers in North America. Of the 25 honorees for 2026, 40 percent hold GBAC STAR accreditation — a performance-based certification administered by the Global Biorisk Advisory Council focused on cleaning rigor, bio-risk management, and infectious disease prevention. Another 56 percent carry LEED certification from the U.S. Green Building Council. Seven venues hold both.

## What These Certifications Actually Require

GBAC STAR is not a self-reported credential. Facilities seeking accreditation must demonstrate evidence-based cleaning and disinfection protocols, formal bio-risk management programs, verified staff training, emergency outbreak preparedness, and ongoing third-party auditing. The standard was designed in response to the COVID-19 pandemic and has since become a benchmark for large venues that host thousands of people from around the world in close proximity.

LEED certification addresses the building itself — how it was designed and how it is operated — with measurable outcomes in indoor air quality, ventilation effectiveness, energy efficiency, water conservation, and responsible materials selection. For event planners navigating corporate sustainability commitments and ESG reporting requirements, a LEED-certified venue provides verifiable data rather than vague claims.

The seven facilities that hold both designations in 2026 span geography and scale: Baird Center in Milwaukee, Charlotte Convention Center, Greater Columbus Convention Center, Hawaii Convention Center, McCormick Place in Chicago, Seattle Convention Center, and the Walter E. Washington Convention Center in Washington, D.C.

## Why Planners Are Paying Attention

The shift in buyer behavior is practical as much as philosophical. An event that draws international attendees carries reputational exposure if an infectious disease outbreak is traced back to the venue. A venue that cannot demonstrate its cleaning protocols and air quality management is increasingly a liability, not just a gap in amenity.

At the same time, large organizations are under growing pressure to align event spending with their published sustainability commitments. A venue without a third-party verified environmental record can complicate that alignment. For the convention industry, health and sustainability certifications have quietly become a form of procurement infrastructure — a baseline expectation that the leading venues are now designing their operations to meet.

# Raleigh Promotes From Within as Its Convention Center Prepares to Grow

The Raleigh Convention Center has named Lori Shumpert its new Director of Events — a promotion that lands as the facility enters one of the most consequential periods in its history.

Shumpert joined the center in 2022 as an Event Manager and was elevated to Senior Event Manager in 2025. She brings more than 18 years of experience across large-scale event management, premium services, and venue operations to a role she officially assumed on April 4. The convention center is part of The Complex, Raleigh's broader collection of performing arts and convention venues, which also includes the Martin Marietta Center for the Performing Arts, the Red Hat Amphitheater, and the Coastal Credit Union Music Park at Walnut Creek.

## Stepping Up at a Critical Moment

The timing of the appointment is significant. The Raleigh Convention Center is in the early stages of a $387.5 million expansion that will add approximately 298,000 square feet of meeting and event space to the existing 500,000-square-foot facility — a roughly 60 percent increase in capacity, targeted to open in 2028. The expansion will include a 50,000-square-foot flexible event hall, an 18,000-square-foot ballroom, and 13 additional meeting rooms, with the two buildings connected by glass skybridges.

To make room for the new construction, the Red Hat Amphitheater — which had occupied the expansion site as a temporary venue for nearly two decades — is being relocated one block south. The new amphitheater, designed with a capacity of around 6,000 to 7,000 seats, is expected to open in time for the 2026 performance season. An Omni Hotels property with roughly 600 guest rooms and 55,000 square feet of meeting space is also planned for the district, expected to open in 2027.

## The Case for Internal Promotion

Executive Director Kerry Painter, who has overseen The Complex through the planning and launch of the expansion, cited Shumpert's ability to operate both strategically and with precision as central to the decision. Shumpert is also active in the International Association of Venue Managers, having co-chaired the 2025 IAVM Super Regional in Raleigh and accepted an invitation to speak at the 2026 edition.

For a venue that has been running out of available dates — turning away conventions it cannot accommodate — having an experienced events leader in place as new capacity comes online is less a formality than a foundation for what comes next.

# A Parking Garage That Could Remake Eureka's Downtown Skyline — If the Grant Comes Through

A quiet stretch of downtown Eureka could, within a few years, be dominated by an eight-story structure unlike anything else on the North Coast. Humboldt Transit Authority and the City of Eureka are in the early stages of exploring a grant opportunity that would fund a multi-story parking facility with a convention center on the top floor — and at that height, it would easily stand as the tallest occupied building north of Santa Rosa.

The project is still in a preliminary feasibility phase. A consultant has been retained to produce early conceptual designs and assess whether the proposal meets the criteria for a grant application to the California State Transportation Agency's Transit and Intercity Rail Capital Program, with applications due May 18. Nothing has been approved or committed to, and the designs shared publicly have already been revised once to add more parking stalls and increase bike storage.

## What the Building Would Actually Be

The concept calls for an eight-story structure on the northeast corner of the city block bounded by Third, Fourth, F, and G streets — directly across from the historic Carson Block Building and adjacent to the site where the Eureka Regional Transit & Housing Center, known as the EaRTH Center, is slated to begin construction this spring.

The proposed building would contain 216 parking stalls across multiple floors, ground-level bike storage and retail space, and an 8,500-square-foot convention center on the top floor. One of the more striking design elements: G Street would be narrowed to a single northbound lane between Third and Fourth, with the upper floors of the structure cantilevered above what is currently the southbound lane.

Despite the scale of the concept, city and transit officials were careful to frame the building's purpose. This would not primarily be a convenience facility for downtown shoppers or tourists. The intent is a park-and-ride — a first-mile and last-mile hub designed to reduce overall vehicle miles traveled by giving people a place to leave their cars and connect to bikes, ride-shares, or bus routes to destinations like Cal Poly Humboldt and the airport.

## The Grant Logic

That distinction matters because the TIRCP funding it would pursue is specifically oriented toward reducing vehicle miles traveled and expanding transit connectivity. The feasibility study now underway will need to demonstrate both that the structure would deliver meaningful VMT reductions and that it could be operated on a cost-neutral basis — otherwise, HTA says, there is no point in applying.

Transit agencies across the state are increasingly being asked to diversify their revenue streams rather than rely entirely on public subsidies. The rentable convention space on the top floor, along with potential retail leases on the ground floor, would give HTA an income stream that could help offset operating costs — a model other transit agencies have used at park-and-ride facilities elsewhere in California.

"Other transit agencies own and operate park and rides to bolster ridership and diversify revenue," HTA Deputy General Manager Katie Collender said. "This study will help us understand if one would make sense in Eureka."

## A Changed Skyline

If the grant is awarded and the project moves forward, Eureka would have a new vertical landmark. City Manager Miles Slattery noted that at eight stories, the structure would likely be the tallest occupied building between Santa Rosa and the Oregon border — exceeding the historic Victorian commercial buildings that currently define the downtown profile by a considerable margin. The only local structure that comes close is the idle boiler building at the former Samoa Pulp Mill, which stands around 200 feet but has been out of use for years.

Whether the proposal survives the feasibility process, clears the grant competition, and ultimately gets built remains to be seen. But for a downtown that has been waiting a long time for reinvestment, the conversation itself marks a shift in what Eureka is willing to imagine for its core.
