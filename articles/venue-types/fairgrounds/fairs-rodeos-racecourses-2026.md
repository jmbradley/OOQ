# New Orleans' Second-Oldest Racetrack Almost Closed Last Year. Now It's Rebuilding.

There are not many businesses in America that can claim 154 consecutive years of continuous operation. Fair Grounds Race Course in Mid-City New Orleans is one of them — and after coming closer than it has in decades to shutting down entirely, the track is heading into the 2026 season with a fresh coat of paint, a renovated paddock, and a renewed sense of purpose.

The near-closure stemmed from a 2025 Louisiana Supreme Court ruling that declared historic horse racing machines — a category of slot-style gaming devices that had become a significant revenue pillar for the track and its off-track betting network — unconstitutional. Those machines accounted for close to half of the revenue at Fair Grounds' 13 off-track betting locations. Churchill Downs, Inc., the corporate parent that acquired Fair Grounds in the mid-2000s, began weighing whether to surrender its racing license altogether. An agreement between CDI and the Louisiana Horsemen's Benevolent and Protective Association ultimately kept the track alive.

## A Complex Business in Three Acts

Cathy Beeding, the track's president and general manager, describes Fair Grounds as a business that operates simultaneously across three distinct modes: thoroughbred racing from November through March, a slot facility with roughly 600 machines and an on-site sportsbook at the Gentilly Boulevard location, and a network of 13 off-track betting facilities spread across the region offering year-round simulcast wagering and video poker.

The operation employs around 500 people year-round, swelling to nearly 1,000 during racing season, with roles ranging from cashiers and IT professionals to a full television production crew that broadcasts the racing signal to outlets worldwide. Horse racing, for its part, is the third-largest industry in Louisiana by some measures, behind only oil and gas and tourism — a fact that gives Fair Grounds an economic footprint far larger than its 150 Mid-City acres might suggest.

## Renovation and Recruitment

Despite the financial turbulence, Fair Grounds pushed through a substantial round of improvements this season. A new paddock and winner's circle, renovated seating and flooring throughout the Clubhouse, a refreshed bar, and the creation of the Louisiana Derby Hall have collectively given the venue a more welcoming feel. The paddock renovation alone cost close to half a million dollars and addressed long-standing drainage and surface issues.

The track's recruiting strategy relies on three pillars: the quality of its racing surface, the availability of winter turf racing, and the simple fact that it operates in New Orleans during Mardi Gras season — an argument that, by most accounts, is hard to beat. This season features 57 stakes races with $8.75 million in total purse money. The crown jewel is the Louisiana Derby, now in its 113th edition, which offers a $1 million purse over a distance of one mile and three-sixteenths — the longest prep-race distance on the Kentucky Derby calendar.

## The Jazz Fest Complication

Fair Grounds is also the only racetrack in the country that must completely rebuild its turf course every year. The reason is the New Orleans Jazz & Heritage Festival, which has called the track home for more than 50 years. The transformation from racetrack to concert venue and back again happens in a matter of weeks each spring — a logistical feat that the track's leadership describes as one of the more remarkable behind-the-scenes stories in American entertainment. The turf is torn up, Jazz Fest happens, and then the track maintenance crew rebuilds it from scratch before the next racing season begins.

# After Scandal and a Full Board Overhaul, the Bradford County Fair Is Back Open

The Bradford Agricultural Fair in Starke, Florida opened its 2026 season this week — a quieter milestone than it might sound, given that the fair's very existence was in question just a year ago.

The county-run fair spent much of 2025 under a cloud of controversy involving allegations of financial mismanagement, disputes over land ownership arrangements, and accusations that board members had violated Florida's Sunshine Law, which requires government bodies to conduct business in public. The fallout was significant enough that Bradford County commissioners stepped in and appointed 21 new members to govern the fairgrounds' operations — a near-complete reset of the institution's leadership.

## A Fresh Start Under New Management

Harlee Hutchinson took over as fair manager under the new board and has been working to distance the event from its recent reputation. The changes she highlights are practical as much as symbolic: open board meetings, a new midway company running the rides, and a deliberate effort to bring a different atmosphere to the grounds.

"I understand that there has been sort of a stigma around our Bradford County Fair the last couple years," Hutchinson said, "but we have all worked extremely hard over the last couple months to make this a better experience."

The 2026 season runs through March 8. The opening weekend featured senior citizen discounts, a gospel performance from local band Backwood Boys, and the traditional livestock shows that have long defined the agricultural fair experience across rural North Florida.

## What a Local Fair Means to a Small County

Bradford County, with a population of roughly 28,000, is one of Florida's smaller counties, and the fair is one of its most visible annual civic events. The controversy of the past year did real damage to the institution's standing in the community — the kind of reputational harm that takes more than one good season to repair. For the new board and management, this year's fair is as much about demonstrating basic competence and transparency as it is about livestock ribbons and carnival rides.

Whether the reset holds, and whether the community extends its trust to the new leadership, will become clearer as the week progresses. But the gates are open, the rides are running, and by the account of the people running it, the mood inside is considerably lighter than it was twelve months ago.

# Fort Hays State Is Throwing Open the Gates for Its 60th Rodeo — Free of Charge

The Fort Hays State University Rodeo Club is marking its 60th annual NIRA Rodeo this week with a gesture that puts the milestone in plain terms: admission is free for everyone, no exceptions.

The event runs April 16 through 18 at the Ellis County Fairgrounds in Hays, Kansas, drawing an expected 500 to 600 contestants from roughly 20 colleges and universities across the Central Plains Region, with competitors from Kansas and Oklahoma representing some of the strongest intercollegiate rodeo programs in the country.

## Six Decades of Collegiate Rodeo in Western Kansas

The FHSU Rodeo Club has been operating since 1964, providing student-athletes with structured competition through the National Intercollegiate Rodeo Association. What began as a campus club has grown into one of the premier regional collegiate rodeo events on the NIRA calendar — large enough that it outgrew its former home at the Doug Philip Arena and relocated to the Ellis County Fairgrounds last year to accommodate continued growth.

"The 60th annual rodeo is a special milestone for our program," said Trever Meier, FHSU Rodeo Coach. "We are excited to open our gates to the community at no cost this year and invite everyone to come experience the energy, tradition, and high-level competition that collegiate rodeo has to offer."

## What Spectators Can Expect

The event features a full slate of both roughstock events — bull riding, saddle bronc, bareback — and timed events including barrel racing, team roping, and tie-down roping. Results at the NIRA regional level feed directly into national qualification standings, meaning the stakes for competitors are genuine even as the atmosphere for spectators is welcoming and accessible.

For the Hays community, the free admission is an invitation to experience collegiate rodeo up close without the barrier of a ticket price. For the student-athletes competing, it means performing in front of a full house at a venue that has the space to hold one.

# A State Fair on the National Mall: America's 250th Birthday Plans Take Shape

This summer, the National Mall in Washington, D.C. will become something it has never been before: a state fair. For 16 days between June 25 and July 10, the stretch of civic ground running from the U.S. Capitol to the Washington Monument will host the Great American State Fair, a World's Fair-scale event featuring exhibits from all 56 U.S. states and territories, a 110-foot Ferris wheel, livestock competitions, rodeo events, and Beaux-Arts-style pavilions organized around themes like "Made in America."

The event is organized by Freedom 250, a nonprofit group operating under White House direction, and represents the culmination of a year-long initiative that President Trump launched at the Iowa State Fairgrounds on the eve of Independence Day 2025. Since then, more than 35 states have incorporated America 250 programming into their own fairs and fairgrounds ahead of the National Mall finale.

## Two Organizations, One Anniversary

The Great American State Fair exists alongside — but separately from — the America250 Commission, a nonpartisan body created by Congress to plan the official signature events for the semiquincentennial. The two organizations have emphasized alignment around shared goals, though their distinct origins reflect broader political currents around how to mark the country's 250th birthday.

Congress allocated $150 million to the Department of the Interior in 2025 for anniversary celebrations, with $100 million directed to the Freedom 250 effort. Interior Secretary Doug Burgum framed the National Mall event as a rare opportunity for the country's most recognized public space to function as a gathering point for the full breadth of American regional culture and identity.

## The Fair as Form

The choice of the state fair format is deliberate. State and county fairs have served as civic anchors in American life since the 19th century — places where agricultural heritage, local competition, and community gathering converge. By bringing that format to the National Mall, and doing so at national scale, the event is reaching for a specific kind of American symbolism: accessible, participatory, and rooted in the land rather than in formal ceremony.

The programming reflects that ambition. Alongside the state pavilions and the Ferris wheel, the schedule includes rodeo events, livestock competitions, and five national pavilions organized around American innovation and manufacturing themes. The event concludes on July 10, six days after Independence Day itself, when a separate mass celebration on the Mall is planned featuring an address by President Trump and what organizers have described as the largest pyrotechnics display in U.S. history.
