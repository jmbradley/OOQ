## Inter Miami Finally Has a Home — And It's Pure Miami

After six years playing in a temporary venue in Fort Lauderdale, Inter Miami CF opened the doors to Nu Stadium on Saturday night. The $350 million, 26,700-seat pitch in Miami delivered a 2-2 draw against Austin FC — and a statement that the club co-owned by David Beckham and Jorge Mas has arrived.

The opening wasn't without drama. The City of Miami hadn't issued a temporary certificate of occupancy by gametime, so the match kicked off under a limited event permit with additional restrictions. Some areas of the stadium still aren't finished. But fans didn't seem to mind.

Nu Stadium is the centerpiece of Miami Freedom Park, a privately funded, 131-acre, $1.3 billion development near Miami International Airport. The venue offers panoramic views of downtown and sits steps from public transit — a major selling point with the FIFA World Cup arriving in Miami this June.

The details are distinctly Miami. Marc Anthony performed the National Anthem. A post-match set from Grammy-winning DJ Cedric Gervais kept the crowd around. The team store spans 11,000 square feet over two levels — the largest in MLS — featuring over 40 footwear styles, custom jersey printing, and a Funko experience complete with a life-size Pop! Messi statue.

Inter Miami is one of three MLS clubs opening soccer-specific venues in the coming years, with New York City FC and Chicago Fire FC following in 2027 and 2028. The timing couldn't be better — World Cup summer starts in two months.

## The Rays' $2.3 Billion New Stadium Plan Just Got Real

The Tampa Bay Rays have released a memorandum of understanding that lays out the framework for a new $2.3 billion stadium near Hillsborough Community College's Dale Mabry campus. If everything holds, the team could be playing in a new home by Opening Day 2029.

The funding is a public-private split. Hillsborough County would contribute $750 million, the City of Tampa $251 million, and the Rays would cover the rest — including any cost overruns. Public dollars would come from tourist development taxes, community investment taxes, and local redevelopment funds rather than general taxpayer revenue. That said, the legality of using community investment tax money is already being questioned by several county commissioners.

Under the proposed deal, the county would own the stadium site while a Rays-controlled entity would lease and operate it for 35 years, with options to extend. The team would keep revenue from tickets, concessions, sponsorships, and naming rights. In return, they'd handle maintenance, upgrades, and daily operations — plus carry the insurance, a notable detail given that Tropicana Field's roof was destroyed by a hurricane.

The project is pitched as more than a ballpark. Plans include a surrounding mixed-use development with public parks and plazas, a community benefits plan with local hiring and living wage commitments, and emergency-use access for public officials.

Nothing is final. The deal still needs approval from local government boards, finalized financing agreements, and a sign-off from Major League Baseball. Leaders are targeting June 1 for final agreements to keep the 2029 timeline alive.

## Nashville's New Nissan Stadium Is 75% Done — And It Shows

The new Nissan Stadium on Nashville's East Bank hit 75 percent completion at the end of March, with roughly 2,000 workers on site daily pushing toward a February 2027 opening. The Titans are expected to begin playing there that fall.

The $2.2 billion enclosed stadium is taking shape fast. Nearly all of the curtain wall glass cladding is in place — 1,140 of 1,185 panels installed — giving the building its signature look: floor-to-ceiling windows offering views of the Nashville skyline from inside an enclosed venue. The first bi-fold doors that will open onto outdoor terraces, creating an indoor/outdoor experience, are nearly ready for installation.

Up top, crews have laid out the top and bottom cables for the ETFE roofing system and are preparing to connect them to the perimeter compression ring. Hydraulic lifts will hoist the cables into position over the coming months, a process set to begin in April.

Inside, the details are catching up. Over 50,000 of 52,000 square feet of concrete masonry blocks are complete. Ceramic tiling has started in restrooms across the event, plaza, and main concourse levels. On the food side, 40 of 108 walk-in coolers are installed, with cooking hoods also going in. Thirty-four of 71 fan entry doors are in place, and Daktronics crews are building out the subframing for the stadium's LED boards.

Concerts and other events are expected to precede the Titans' first season in the building. For Nashville, the East Bank skyline is about to change permanently.

## Royals Fans Are Split on a Downtown Stadium — But Ready for an Answer

The Kansas City City Council introduced a proposal Thursday to bring a new Royals stadium downtown, and the reaction outside Kauffman Stadium before that night's game was a mix of excitement and nostalgia.

For some fans, the move is overdue. The Truman Sports Complex never developed the surrounding neighborhood the way planners envisioned, and a downtown ballpark could mean bigger crowds and more energy on game nights. One fan predicted attendance jumping from 5,000 to 15,000–20,000 a game with a downtown location. Others called it exactly what Kansas City baseball needs.

But not everyone is ready to leave. Lifelong fans pointed to decades of memories at Kauffman — championship teams, All-Star moments, traditions built over a lifetime. One 50-year-old fan called the potential departure tragic, especially after the city already lost the Chiefs. He raised concerns about the economic impact on the area around the current complex.

The players, for their part, are staying out of it. First baseman Vinnie Pasquantino said he hadn't even heard the news until a reporter told him. All-Star shortstop Bobby Witt Jr. kept it simple: he just wants to win a championship while the team is still at Kauffman.

The Royals organization released a statement thanking city leadership for taking steps toward continued economic development and said they look forward to more detailed conversations about the team's future home.

No timeline, no final deal — just movement. And for a fan base that's been waiting on resolution, even that felt like progress.
