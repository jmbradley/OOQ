# At Coachella, the Real Work Happens Before Anyone Takes the Stage

Every April, the Instagram grid fills with the same images: flower crowns, golden-hour portraits, and casually perfect outfits against a desert backdrop. What those images rarely show is the spreadsheet sitting open on someone's laptop two weeks earlier — full of shot lists, posting schedules, and brand deliverable deadlines.

That gap between appearance and reality is the defining tension of Coachella's relationship with the creator economy. The festival has become as much a professional proving ground for social media talent as it is a music event. Creators arrive with content calendars mapped out in advance, Airbnbs chosen for their visual suitability, and partnership agreements already signed. Some attend without a ticket in hand, betting that posting publicly about the hustle will itself attract an invitation from a brand willing to trade access for exposure — a gamble that sometimes pays off within days.

## The Economics Behind the Feed

The monetization paths are surprisingly layered. Platform shopping features on TikTok and YouTube turn festival outfit content into direct commission revenue. Brand deals can cover travel and accommodation, though they often come with restrictions on what else a creator can post or promote. Even going independently, without a sponsorship, can pencil out financially if a creator's Coachella videos generate enough engagement to offset the trip's cost.

For fashion and beauty creators especially, Coachella is one of the most reliable content calendars of the year. Audiences are actively seeking outfit and makeup inspiration in the weeks before, during, and after the festival — and the demand is consistent enough that creators plan for it months in advance, much like a retailer plans for a seasonal sale.

## The Show Within the Show

YouTube, which livestreams Coachella and considers it its flagship music event, has watched the creator presence grow to the point where cameras trained on the audience now nearly rival those pointed at the performers. The festival and the documentation of the festival have become almost inseparable — a dual broadcast that serves both the 125,000 people in the desert and the millions watching from home.

For creators, the goal isn't just the weekend itself. The content window extends weeks in both directions: the anticipation, the live posts, and the retrospective recap videos all carry their own audience and their own revenue. Coachella, for this community, isn't a festival you attend. It's a project you execute.

# The Ferris Wheel That Became Coachella's Most Photographed Landmark Has a Secret Life

Open almost any Coachella photo from the past decade and somewhere in the frame — or front and center — you'll find it: a towering wheel of light glowing against the Indio mountains at dusk. It has become as synonymous with the festival as the lineup itself, a landmark that requires no caption to place.

What most of those photos don't convey is the sheer mechanical effort required to make it appear.

## Built to Travel, Designed to Belong

The wheel, formally called La Grande XL and operated by Ray Cammack Shows, travels to the Empire Polo Club each year aboard 20 semitrucks. A crew of ten workers then spends five full days bringing it upright with the help of a 70-ton crane — all to create what looks, in a well-timed photograph, like it simply belongs there.

The current version of the wheel, which replaced an earlier model in 2017, holds 36 air-conditioned gondolas and is studded with more than half a million LED lights. Between county fairs and rodeos across the country, La Grande XL keeps a busy calendar year-round, stopping at events like the Houston Livestock Show and Rodeo and the LA County Fair.

## The Finishing Touch That Stays in California

One feature never leaves Southern California: a custom-designed centerpiece bearing a palm tree and roadrunner logo, built exclusively for Coachella and the adjacent Stagecoach festival. Installing that logo alone takes six hours and is always the final step before the wheel is considered ready — the finishing detail that completes the entire structure. When the festivals are over, the logo stays behind in Southern California, waiting for the next season.

The wheel is more than a backdrop. It functions as a landmark and meeting point on grounds where 125,000 people can lose each other within minutes. A standard ten-minute ride runs $15. A private gondola for up to six people is $80. The view, by most accounts, is the best in the desert.

# 2026 Is Shaping Up to Be One of the Most Stacked Festival Years in Recent Memory

Whatever kind of live music fan you are, 2026 is making a strong case that you belong at a festival. The year's calendar has swelled into a genuine sprawl — dozens of events spread across every region of the country, covering genres that have little in common beyond the shared logic of a stage, a crowd, and a weekend to remember.

The sheer range is the headline.

## Something for Every Genre

Country devotees have Stagecoach in Indio and the Barefoot Country Music Festival on the beaches of Wildwood, New Jersey, where Miranda Lambert, Kelsea Ballerini, Post Malone, and Eric Church are set to headline across more than 40 acts. Rock and metal fans are well served by Sonic Temple in Columbus — now sprawling across five stages and 140-plus bands — and Bourbon & Beyond in Louisville, which pairs Foo Fighters and Mumford & Sons with Chris Stapleton and Dave Matthews Band over four days in late September.

EDM has Ultra in Miami and the nationally touring Breakaway festival, expanding this year to a record 14 cities including new stops in Houston and Salt Lake City. The nostalgia circuit is well-represented too, with When We Were Young and Sick New World catering to the early-2000s rock faithful.

## The Anchors of the Season

Bonnaroo returns to its Tennessee farm in June with Skrillex, The Strokes, RÜFÜS DU SOL, and Noah Kahan headlining across four days and more than ten stages. BottleRock Napa blends Foo Fighters and Lorde with wine tastings and chef demonstrations over Memorial Day weekend. And Coachella — in its 25th edition — drew Sabrina Carpenter, Justin Bieber, and Karol G to the Empire Polo Club for a pair of sold-out weekends in April.

If there's a unifying theme to 2026's festival landscape, it's abundance. The question is no longer whether there's something for you — it's figuring out which weekends to actually block off.

# SXSW Is Looking for Its Next Chapter — and a New Leader to Write It

South by Southwest has spent the better part of 40 years becoming one of the world's most recognizable gatherings for music, film, and technology. Now it finds itself in the middle of something it has rarely faced: genuine uncertainty about what it wants to be next.

The clearest signal came last year when Hugh Forrest — who first walked through the door in 1989 and had shaped the festival's programming identity for most of his tenure — departed after learning he would not be elevated to the CEO role. In his place, Penske Media Corporation installed Jenny Connelly, the company's head of product and technology, who had been serving on the SXSW board since 2021. She arrived with a corporate background in digital strategy and a prior stint at Live Nation, bringing a very different profile than the programming-obsessed culture-builders who built the event from scratch.

## A Festival in Transition

The numbers tell part of the story. Attendance in 2025 sat at roughly 309,000 — nearly 110,000 fewer than the pre-pandemic peak. The Austin Convention Center that anchored the festival since 1993 has been demolished, replaced by a construction site that won't reopen until 2029. The Innovation Awards, a longtime fixture of the conference, were quietly cancelled for the 2026 edition.

The structural response has been significant. The schedule shrank from nine days to seven, with the music, film, and innovation tracks running concurrently for the first time in the festival's history. Badge prices came down. And with the convention center gone, organizers shifted to a clubhouse model — distinct hubs scattered across downtown Austin venues — echoing the decentralized setup piloted at SXSW London.

## The Question Ahead

Connelly has been direct about the challenge, acknowledging that some longtime attendees feel the festival lost its creative edge years ago. Her task is to separate that nostalgia from the concrete feedback — and to determine which parts of SXSW's identity are worth preserving, and which need to evolve.

Whether a more focused, leaner SXSW can rebuild the sense of cultural electricity that once made it unmissable is the question its new leadership is now tasked with answering. For a festival that once launched Twitter into the mainstream and made Austin feel like the center of the cultural universe for two weeks every March, the stakes of getting that answer right are considerable.
