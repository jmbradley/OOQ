## Vail's Iconic Amphitheater Swaps Its Orange Seats for a $19 Million Makeover

The Gerald R. Ford Amphitheater in Vail debuted brand-new pavilion seating this weekend as the Après at The Amp music festival brought Tiësto, Purple Disco Machine, and LP Giobbi to the mountains for two nights of electronic music on April 10–11.

Festival-goers were the first to sit in the new myrtle green seats — upholstered with cushions and cup holders, 100% fabricated in the United States, and replacing the original orange seats installed by Irwin Seating Company back in 1983. The swap is the first visible piece of a $19 million multi-year transformation of one of Colorado's most iconic outdoor venues.

Phase one also included concrete repairs, fresh paint, no-slip texturing, an expanded production booth, and new hand railings. By May 2028, the full renovation will deliver remodeled concessions, enhanced green rooms and dressing rooms, box office upgrades, a new multi-use studio and event space, expanded storage, and improvements to lighting, acoustics, and office spaces.

The upgrades are part of the Vail Valley Foundation's $74 million Empowering Possibility Capital Campaign, which supports arts, athletics, education, and community services across the Eagle River Valley. The campaign has already raised more than $40 million toward its goal.

All construction is being phased during winter off-seasons to avoid disrupting summer performance schedules — including the Bravo! Vail Music Festival and Vail Dance Festival. The goal: position The Amp to attract the world's biggest artists while meeting modern production and guest experience standards.

## Cleveland's Riverfront Is Getting a 6,200-Seat Amphitheater

Bedrock Real Estate and Live Nation have submitted preliminary plans for a 6,200-seat outdoor amphitheater on Cleveland's downtown riverfront — a new addition to Bedrock's $3.5 billion, 40-acre Flats South redevelopment.

The venue would sit on 4.1 acres at West 3rd Street and Stones Levee Road, oriented so audiences see the Cuyahoga River's Collision Bend and the downtown skyline as a backdrop behind live performances. The design includes upper and lower bowl seating with separate entrances and amenities for general admission and VIP ticket holders. VIPs would get their own outdoor dining area, food and beverage counters, and even dedicated restrooms.

A stagehouse will surround and protect the stage, with gantries for lighting, sound, and pyrotechnics. Backstage areas include artist suites, crew quarters, and loading zones for trucks and buses. One key design element still being defined: a canopy over the lower bowl that could become a signature architectural feature, similar to the Acrisure Amphitheater in Grand Rapids or Blossom Music Center's iconic sloping roof in the Cuyahoga Valley.

To make way for the project, two vacant warehouses totaling over 22,000 square feet are being demolished at an estimated cost of $350,000. The amphitheater likely replaces Bedrock's previously proposed $500 million Rock & Roll Land hotel and theater complex on Huron Road, though a hotel component is still in the works.

The venue would compete with the 4,153-seat Jacobs Pavilion farther down the river — and revive a tradition: a 5,000-seat amphitheater stood across Stones Levee from 2001 to 2012 before being demolished for casino parking.

## Willmar, Minnesota, Is Building a Two-Stage Amphitheater on an Island

The Willmar City Council approved plans and authorized bids for a new amphitheater at Robbins Island Regional Park, greenlit for construction this summer. What started as a grassroots vision from the local Rotary Club in 2022 is finally becoming real.

The two-stage design is the standout. A 1,536-square-foot main stage faces outward to accommodate crowds of up to 10,000 — elevated several feet above ground for sightlines. Flip to the back and there's a 626-square-foot intimate stage nestled into the park's natural landscape, sized for about 500 people and ideal for weddings, graduations, theater, community bands, and church services. Barn doors between the two stages can open to create a single event space, and doors for stage left and right can accommodate anything from rock concerts to theater productions.

The building features an iconic butterfly roof designed by Engan Associates, with exposed beams that project performances toward the audience and integrated lighting that will make the structure glow at night. Storage, a green room with air conditioning, and a performer restroom round out the interior. Materials will match the existing park structures, and an ADA-compliant concrete path and plaza will ensure accessibility.

The project is funded by approximately $2.6 million in grants from the Greater Minnesota Regional Parks and Trails Commission, $250,000 from the Rotary Club, and additional community contributions. Construction won't disrupt the Rockin' Robbins summer concert series.

## North Las Vegas Unveils a Renovated Amphitheater at Craig Ranch Park

North Las Vegas opened its newly renovated amphitheater at Craig Ranch Regional Park on Friday night with a free concert: "Totally 80s Symphonic," featuring a rock band and orchestra performing iconic hits from the decade.

The upgrades include a larger stage area, a new screen, and new sound equipment — improvements designed to expand the venue's capacity for concerts, shows, and family-friendly programming. Mayor Pamela Goynes-Brown said the city plans to bring events to the amphitheater nearly every weekend, with a dedicated promoter already booking programming.

Craig Ranch Regional Park is located near Craig Road and Martin Luther King Boulevard in North Las Vegas. The renovated amphitheater adds to a string of recent park improvements in the area, positioning the venue as a growing community gathering space in the northern Las Vegas Valley.
