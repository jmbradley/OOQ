# The Casino Map Is Getting New Pins in 2026 — From Oklahoma to Queens

The American casino landscape has always been more geographically distributed than its popular mythology suggests. Las Vegas gets the headlines, but the country's gambling infrastructure stretches from tribal lands in rural Oklahoma to racetracks in Virginia, and 2026 is adding several new names to that map. Five openings this year reflect how broadly casino gaming has spread — and how much the format has evolved beyond rows of slot machines and a buffet.

## Oklahoma Gets Another Destination

The Coweta Casino Hotel, which opened in March about 30 minutes southeast of Tulsa, is a $100 million property built by the Muscogee Nation on 104,000 square feet of land. The development arrived fully formed: more than 500 slots, a 46-room hotel, and three dining options — a grab-and-go Italian counter, a full-service bar and grill, and a cocktail lounge. Oklahoma already has more than 100 casinos statewide, but the Coweta property represents a new scale of investment for a community that had previously had more limited gaming options.

## Virginia Goes All-In

Live! Casino opened in Petersburg, Virginia in January under a tent — a temporary structure holding over 900 slot machines and 30 table games while a permanent facility is built for a 2027 opening. Virginia only launched its first casino in 2023, but revenues have grown quickly enough to support continued expansion. The Petersburg permanent project will include a 200-room hotel and a full sports book, positioning the city as an entertainment destination for a region that previously had to travel to Maryland or Atlantic City for major gaming.

## California and Nevada Fill Gaps

In California's Gold Country, the Acorn Ridge Casino opened in February as a 60,000-square-foot tribal property in Plymouth — a small wine country town with limited entertainment options. The casino's 480 slots, outdoor event space, and Stone Creek Kitchen restaurant give locals and visitors an experience that previously required a multi-hour drive. In Henderson, Nevada, Cadence Crossing replaced the older Jokers Wild casino in March with a freshly built slot parlor — no table games, but more than 450 machines and two restaurant concepts, including a 24-hour taqueria.

## The Biggest Story: New York City

None of 2026's casino stories carries more national weight than Resorts World New York City, which has operated since 2011 as a video lottery terminal facility but is now beginning a multi-billion-dollar transformation. The first phase, launching in spring 2026, adds live table games for the first time — hundreds of them, with real dealers and chips replacing the purely electronic experience. The longer-term plan is sweeping: more than 6,000 slot machines, over 800 table games, a 2,000-room Hyatt expansion, a 7,000-person entertainment venue, and 12 acres of public green space. If fully realized, Resorts World NYC would rank among the largest casinos in the country — in the middle of Queens.

# Wyndham Just Added Nearly 2,000 Rooms — and a Lot of Oklahoma — to Its Portfolio

Wyndham Hotels & Resorts entered 2026 with a deal that does something its typical franchising activity rarely accomplishes: adding properties that are genuinely destination-worthy in their own right. A new multi-hotel agreement with the Choctaw Nation of Oklahoma brings four Choctaw Casino & Resorts properties — representing nearly 2,000 rooms in total — into the Wyndham family, with the Nation retaining full ownership and management of all four.

The flagship of the deal is Choctaw Casino & Resort in Durant, which now carries the Wyndham Grand flag. Located 90 minutes north of Dallas, the Durant property is one of the largest resort complexes in the South-Central United States: more than 1,600 rooms across multiple towers, a gaming floor with over 7,400 slot machines and 100 table games, a 3,000-seat entertainment venue, more than 40 restaurants and bars, 100,000 square feet of meeting and event space, a full-service spa, a collection of pools, and a family entertainment center with 20 bowling lanes, arcade games, and a six-screen cinema. Three additional Choctaw properties — in Hochatown, Pocola, and Grant — join Wyndham's Trademark Collection.

## What Each Side Gets

For Wyndham, the deal expands its upscale and lifestyle portfolio with properties that bring built-in demand from a large and loyal regional guest base. The Choctaw Nation's resorts are not untested concepts — they are established operations with demonstrated traffic. Adding them to the Wyndham Rewards program, which has more than 120 million enrolled members, extends their visibility to a national and international traveler base.

For the Choctaw Nation, the arrangement offers distribution and technology infrastructure — including next-generation property management systems and centralized marketing support backed by more than $375 million in Wyndham technology investment since 2018 — without ceding operational control. Choctaw Chief Gary Batton framed it in explicitly economic and community terms, describing the partnership as a way to strengthen career pathways and professional development for tribal members while reinvesting in tribal programs.

The properties are rolling out across Wyndham's booking channels and will be available to Wyndham Business members as well.

# The All-Inclusive Resort Has Grown Up. Two Tennessee and Arizona Properties Are Leading the Way.

For a long time, the all-inclusive resort existed in American travel as a budget-adjacent concept — a cruise ship on land, built around mass catering and bundled activities of middling quality. The 2026 U.S. News & World Report rankings of the best all-inclusive resorts in America tell a different story, and the properties at the top of that list are firmly in the other direction.

Blackberry Farm in Walland, Tennessee and Castle Hot Springs in Arizona's Bradshaw Mountains are the standout names in this year's rankings, and what they share is a model of all-inclusive hospitality that is the opposite of generic. Both are small, intimate properties built around a specific sense of place — one a 4,200-acre working estate at the foot of the Great Smoky Mountains, the other a historic desert retreat with mineral-rich natural springs.

## Why These Properties Lead

At Blackberry Farm, the all-inclusive rate covers three farm-to-table meals daily, wine pairings, beer and spirits, and a wide range of activities from horseback riding and fly-fishing to yoga and paddleboarding. The culinary program, built around what the property calls "Foothills Cuisine" and sourced from a four-acre on-site garden, has earned the farm a reputation as one of the serious American food destinations — not just a nice place to eat between activities. Accommodations run from historic rooms to multi-bedroom houses, and rates start around $1,200 per night with a three-night minimum.

Castle Hot Springs offers a different experience, anchored by three mineral-rich soaking pools whose waters bubble up beneath the property. The resort has a small number of rooms, casitas, and bungalows, and its all-inclusive rate covers breakfast, lunch, and a multicourse dinner — though alcohol is handled via a daily per-guest credit rather than unlimited inclusion. Activities include archery, ax throwing, pickleball, and biking, with guided hikes and e-bike tours also available.

## The Broader Shift

Both properties represent what the U.S. News rankings increasingly reward: all-inclusive experiences defined by deep local identity rather than scale or uniformity. The top-ranked American resorts are ranch properties in Montana, hot spring retreats in the desert, mountain lodges in the Poconos — places where the surroundings are the point, and where guests immerse in a landscape rather than simply being served within one.

# 2026's Best New Hotels Reward Travelers Who Know Where to Look

The Travel + Leisure best new hotels list for 2026 skews toward properties that have been a long time coming — restorations of historic landmarks, first-time entries by storied European operators into the American market, and reopenings of icons that have been closed since the pandemic. What unites most of the year's standout openings is a commitment to specificity: these hotels are designed to feel like they belong exactly where they are, not like they could be anywhere.

## The Vineta Hotel, Palm Beach

The most-discussed American opening of the year is The Vineta Hotel, which reopened in March on Palm Beach's Cocoanut Row after a full renovation of a Mediterranean Revival building that dates to 1926. The project is significant for two reasons: it marks Oetker Collection's first American property, and it deliberately scaled back — from 53 rooms to 41 — to create something more intimate and considered than the typical Palm Beach luxury offering. Paris-based designer Tino Zervudachi led the interiors, working in rattan, pale woods, and palm motifs that feel at once contemporary and historically grounded. The restaurant, Coco's, draws culinary influence from other Oetker properties including Hotel du Cap-Eden-Roc, and the bar operates on a seated-only basis to preserve the atmosphere. The hotel's arrival coincides with Palm Beach tourism at a record high, with visitation up eight percent year over year.

## Four Seasons The Biltmore Santa Barbara

Closed since 2020, the Four Seasons Biltmore in Santa Barbara is expected to reopen in late spring or early summer of this year following extensive renovations. The project includes a new adult pool, a 3,500-square-foot family pool with a lazy river, and a waterfall feature. For a property of its age and standing on the California coast, the reopening is one of the most anticipated hotel events of 2026 for domestic travelers.

## The Waldorf Astoria New York

After a multi-year restoration, the Waldorf Astoria has reopened on Park Avenue with what the brand describes as Manhattan's largest guest rooms, a 30,000-plus square foot spa, and fully restored Art Deco public spaces. The hotel's return carries both cultural and commercial weight — it is one of New York's most historically freighted addresses, and its reopening is a milestone for the city's luxury hospitality recovery.

## What the Pattern Reveals

Across the 2026 list, the hotels generating the most attention are those with a story that precedes them — places with decades of identity that new operators are choosing to honor rather than erase. The Vineta's century-old pink facade, the Biltmore's California coastal legacy, the Waldorf's Park Avenue permanence. In a market where new-build luxury hotels are proliferating rapidly, the properties standing out are often the ones that arrive with history already in place.
