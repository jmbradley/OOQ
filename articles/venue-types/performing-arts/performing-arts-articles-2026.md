# An Alameda Waterfront That's Been Waiting for Its Anchor Might Finally Get One

On the former grounds of Naval Air Station Alameda, where decommissioned runways meet the San Francisco Bay, a project years in the making cleared a significant hurdle in March. The city's Planning Board voted unanimously to approve a development plan amendment for the Radium Theatre Performing Arts Center — a 35,000-square-foot, two-story building with a 550-seat theater, rooftop bar, restaurant, outdoor performance plaza, and waterfront access, proposed for a two-acre site along the Seaplane Lagoon Taxiway.

The vote advances the project to the next stage of regulatory review, though it does not authorize construction. Final design details will return for separate review, and substantial permitting work remains.

## A Design That Evolved to Fit Its Setting

The Radium Theatre has been in various stages of planning since founder Christopher Seiwald — a software executive who has long been an advocate for bringing a serious performing arts facility to Alameda — first proposed the concept years ago. The project has gone through multiple design revisions, each responding to feedback from the Planning Board, the Historical Advisory Board, and the surrounding community.

The most consequential shift was a westward relocation of the main building to preserve historic view corridors from the nearby Alameda Naval Air Museum to the waterfront. That adjustment created a larger public plaza between the theater and the museum while maintaining the open sightlines that define the Point's character. The project also includes an interim parking lot, a new internal roadway, and landscaping designed to integrate ecological and trail connections with the broader Alameda waterfront path network.

The building itself is designed to balance maritime industrial references with contemporary materials — metal panels, concrete, and wood — and to orient its primary activity toward the water rather than the street, with a public plaza and improved shoreline access behind the structure.

## What the Theater Would Mean for Alameda

Alameda is an island city with limited venues for mid-to-large scale performing arts programming. Supporters of the Radium project have consistently described the gap in those terms — not just as an amenity question, but as an infrastructure one. Board member Tara Pilbrow, speaking at the meeting, called it "a symbol of the importance of the arts to this city."

Seiwald framed the stakes plainly: "If it happens, it will be a focal point for Alameda Point — it could define all of Alameda once completed." Whether the project ultimately reaches construction will depend on the remaining approvals, financing, and the continuing development momentum at Alameda Point, where several blocks of housing are already complete.

# RIT Opens a Theater Built for the Whole Campus, Not Just the Arts Students

On April 10, Rochester Institute of Technology cut the ribbon on its new Performing Arts Center — a 747-seat, 50,388-square-foot building designed by Los Angeles-based architect Michael Maltzan that has been years in the making and is now the largest dedicated performance venue on the RIT campus.

The grand opening ceremony was emceed by veteran local anchor Don Alhart and included a discussion with RIT President Bill Sanders and three of his predecessors — Albert Simone, Bill Destler, and David Munson — who reflected on how the project evolved across multiple administrations before reaching completion.

## Designed Around Inclusion, Not Just Performance

The building was designed primarily for musical theater, but with a deliberate flexibility that extends its usefulness across a much wider range of events — university ceremonies, lectures, community productions, and interdisciplinary student projects. That breadth was intentional.

"This theater was designed specifically for musical theater productions, but also to be useable for a whole broad range of other performing arts, as well as university events," said Munson, who served as president from 2017 to 2025 and remains in an emeritus role. He noted that musical theater was chosen as the anchor discipline precisely because of its collaborative demands — it does not require students to be trained performers to participate meaningfully.

"Almost any kind of student on our campus can be involved in productions at the theater in some capacity," Munson said. "There are tons of things backstage — costumes, sets. Nowadays, the sets are actually animated, they move. RIT has one of the top animation programs in the nation."

The first performance in the building — a sold-out 75-minute cabaret-style show featuring 11 acts of RIT students — took place the same evening as the ribbon-cutting. Hundreds of students were involved, both on stage and in production roles.

## Technology Meets Artistry

President Sanders offered a phrase that captured the design philosophy: "This will be a space where technology enhances artistry, where access is foundational and where collaboration thrives." For a university that sits at the intersection of technical and creative education, the combination was both fitting and, for many on campus, long overdue.

# Video Game Music Has Grown Up. Cleveland's Maltz Center Is Proving It Belongs in the Concert Hall.

When the Video Game Symphony takes the stage at the Maltz Performing Arts Center on April 18, it will be the ensemble's second appearance at the storied University Circle venue — a return that signals something more than a single event. It suggests that orchestral video game music has earned a durable place in the performing arts calendar.

The concert, titled "Press Continue," features a live symphony orchestra and choir performing scores from across decades of gaming history: Mario, Zelda, Final Fantasy, Pokémon, Gears of War, Chrono Trigger, Castlevania, Hollow Knight, Donkey Kong Country, and more. The program spans generations and genres deliberately, designed to reach audiences who came to love these works as children as well as those who discovered the tradition later.

## A Northeast Ohio Ensemble Finding Its Home

The Video Game Symphony was founded in Cleveland in April 2023 by Andrew Keller and Jaclyn Kurtz, both rooted in Northeast Ohio's classical music community. Keller, who holds a PhD and also serves as the organization's CEO, credits video game soundtracks — particularly the score to Chrono Trigger — with shaping his entire trajectory as a musician. The ensemble has since performed at the Akron Civic Theatre, Kent State University, and a range of regional gaming and fan conventions, building a following that straddles the classical music world and the gaming community.

The Maltz Performing Arts Center, housed in the restored Temple-Tifereth Israel synagogue in University Circle, has become the ensemble's flagship venue. The center's Silver Hall Concert Series — which offers free concerts and free parking — has made it an accessible entry point for audiences who might not otherwise attend a formal orchestral performance. That alignment between venue mission and ensemble ethos has made the partnership a natural one.

## A Genre That Has Outgrown Its Novelty Phase

Video game music has been performed by major orchestras around the world for years, and the genre's most celebrated composers — Nobuo Uematsu, Koji Kondo, Yoko Shimomura — are now recognized figures in the broader world of contemporary music. What the Video Game Symphony represents is the community level of that movement: a locally organized ensemble bringing the same repertoire to audiences in their own city, in a venue that was not designed for the purpose but has fully embraced it.

The April 18 concert is also available via free livestream for those who cannot attend in person.

# Denver's Broadway Season Is Looking Like One of Its Most Ambitious in Years

The Denver Center for the Performing Arts has announced its 2026–27 Broadway and Cabaret season, and the lineup reads as a confident statement about where the institution — and the city's appetite for live theater — currently stands.

The subscription season opens in late October with Disney's Beauty and the Beast, which is making its first North American touring appearance in more than 25 years. It is joined by a roster that mixes returning audience favorites with a substantial number of productions making their Denver debut, including The Great Gatsby, BOOP! The Musical, Death Becomes Her, and Maybe Happy Ending. The Book of Mormon returns by popular demand, and three of the five shows nominated for Best Musical at the 2025 Tony Awards are included in the season.

## A Season Built Around Range

The programming philosophy is broad by design. John Ekeberg, Executive Director of DCPA Broadway and Cabaret, framed the season around the specific value of live theater over other entertainment formats — the collective anticipation in the room before a show begins, the shared experience that distinguishes a performance from a recording.

"This season underscores the elements that define Broadway at its best — spectacle, storytelling, and the profound emotional journeys that only live theatre can provide," Ekeberg said in a statement. "That collective anticipation — the spark unique to live performance — is something we value deeply."

The added attractions category brings additional returning favorites: Jersey Boys, Waitress, Legally Blonde: The Musical, Mamma Mia!, and Riverdance. The DCPA's own Cabaret wing will produce What a Glorious Feeling!, alongside A Beautiful Noise: The Neil Diamond Musical for the added attractions list.

## What It Means for Denver

The DCPA is one of the largest performing arts organizations in the United States, and its Broadway season functions as a significant barometer of both national touring activity and local cultural engagement. A season of this depth — featuring multiple world-class productions and several Colorado premieres — reflects continued institutional health and audience demand that the organization is working to sustain heading into the second half of the decade.

Full season subscriptions are available now, with single tickets to go on sale at a later date.
